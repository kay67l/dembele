About the Council

The Ashanti Regional Students' Representative Council (ARSRC) is the recognized umbrella body coordinating Students' Representative Councils (SRCs) of senior high schools across the Ashanti Region.

Aims & Objectives

  • ~To constitute a mouthpiece for the interests of second cycle students in the Ashanti Region.
  • ~To ensure a constant flow of information between students and educational authorities
  • ~To offer a common forum for the discussion of students' problems in the Ashanti Region
  • ~To train students in leadership skills not only to be student leaders but also to take up leadership positions in the nation.
  • ~Shall contribute to the formation and the establishment of a National Students' Representative Council for second cycle institutions.
  • ~The council shall seek to influence educational policies as pertaining students by holding regular meetings with stakeholders of education when necessary.
  • ~To ensure the continuous existence and functioning of Students' Representative Councils in all member institutions. This is based on the fact that an effective SRC is an asset to schools' administration

Founded in 1997, the ARSRC is the second oldest regional students' council in Ghana and remains one of the most active, having been recognized as Most Outstanding Regional SRC of the Year at the 2019 NUGS Excellence Awards. Today the Council coordinates 146 member schools across four zones.

Representation

Serving as the collective voice of students before education authorities, school management, and government bodies.

Leadership Development

Building capacity in student leaders through training, mentorship, and inter-school collaboration programs.

Unity Across Schools

Strengthening bonds between member schools through shared initiatives, events, and a common constitution.

ARSRC Constitution

LG By-Laws, WDS By-Laws & Prefects Manual
Amended 2015/2016 · Published 2021

Preamble+

Adopted by the students of the Ashanti Region in recognition of the many challenges facing second-cycle students, the constitution establishes the Council as a pressure group free of social, cultural, political, and academic prejudice — built to channel student energy into productive ventures and act swiftly when the need arises.

Chapter One — Name, Supremacy & Enforcement (Art. 1–3)+

Establishes the legitimate body as the "Ashanti Regional Students Representative Council." This constitution is the supreme law governing the regional and all local SRCs, subject only to the Constitution and laws of Ghana. All members must adhere to its provisions, and officers subscribe to an oath of allegiance upon induction.

Chapter Two — Aims & Objectives (Art. 5)+

The Council exists to be the mouthpiece of second-cycle students in the Ashanti Region, ensure constant information flow between students and educational authorities, offer a forum for discussing student problems, train leaders, contribute to a future National SRC, influence educational policy through regular stakeholder meetings, and sustain functioning SRCs in every member institution.

Chapter Three — Composition & Membership (Art. 6–9)+

The Regional Council comprises all second-cycle GES institutions in the Ashanti Region — Senior High, Technical/Vocational, Commercial, and approved private schools — organized into Local Councils and four Zonal Councils. Local Councils include prefects, SRC representatives, class prefects, and group heads. A Regional Patron (appointed by CHASS) and Local General/Chief Patrons provide oversight and advisory support.

Chapter Four — Functions & Affiliations (Art. 10–11)+

Regional, Zonal, and Local SRCs organize symposia, workshops, and educational visits; help resolve issues facing member institutions; and periodically engage education stakeholders. The Council maintains working relationships with other regional SRCs and with the Association of Past Executives and Coordinators (APEC), which serves in an advisory capacity.

Chapter Five — Officials, Structure & Duties (Art. 12–20)+

Executive Committees operate at Regional (REC), Zonal (ZEC), and Local (LEC) levels. The REC comprises the Regional President, Vice-President, Secretary, and four Trustees who double as Zonal heads. The LEC is led by the Local SRC President (the Senior Prefect) alongside a Vice-President, Secretary, Organizing Secretaries, Financial Secretary, and the Literary Group President. A Permanent Secretariat, run by Regional Coordinators, handles day-to-day administration, finances, and record-keeping.

Chapter Seven — Meetings & Special Events (Art. 21–24)+

At least two Regional General Meetings are held each academic year, with Local SRCs meeting at least twice a term. The Annual Residential Congress — capped at seven days — is the Council's highest decision-making body, receiving executive reports, financial statements, and shaping strategy. The calendar also includes an Annual Students' Week and a Hand-Over & Awards Ceremony marking the transition of executives.

Chapter Eight — Elections (Art. 25–27)+

REC, ZEC, and LIGEC officers are elected at Congress; LEC members (besides the President, who is the Senior Prefect) are elected at a Local General Meeting. Candidates apply via a nomination form co-signed by the local SRC President and patron, with no discrimination on sex, colour, creed, or religion. Terms last one year, renewable for a second term only, and elections are supervised by an Electoral Commission of outgoing officers and APEC representatives.

Chapter Nine & Ten — Structure, Finance & Audit (Art. 28–30)+

Congress sits atop the Council's hierarchy, followed by the Regional General Meeting, REC, ZEC, LIGEC, and Local Councils. Funding comes from termly member dues (split 20% Regional / 80% Local), donations, and income-generating ventures, held in accounts co-signed by the Regional Patron, President, and Director of Education. Accounts are audited annually by the GES Regional Audit team.

Discipline & Amendments (Art. 31–32, 37–38)+

Disciplinary committees operate at every level to investigate conduct that brings the Council into disrepute; removal requires a two-thirds vote of no confidence, and unexplained absence from duty for four months counts as abandonment of office. Proposed amendments need ¾ support at a General Meeting, though select entrenched clauses can only be changed at Congress.
